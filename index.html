A brief description of a few types of encryption employed throughout history. * Notes for reference* This is not a full history of Ciphers throughout the ages, rather the aim of this site is to try and introduce some of the stories and techniques, and to see how it has changed through the years. Target Audience: Perhaps young people who are interested in puzzles and techniques *End of notes for reference* Introduction and background Exciting introduction and brief explanation for the general cipher With the evolution of language and writing, it seems like in every culture there has also been an evolution of the means to secrecy. Ever since humans have been at war with each other, the need to communicate secretly has been important. There has been a constant race between the code makers and the code breakers, like an intellectual arms race. The main focus of this site will be cryptology, which is defined as the science of cryptography and cryptanalysis. Kryptos is the Greek work for to hide, graphein to write and analusis to unloosen. A cipher is a tool used by the cryptographer to create a ciphertext, from the message that the sender wants to send. Using the right Key, the receiver should be able to decipher the message with ease, however it should be difficult to decode.

This is one of the earliest known examples of ciphers in written history, appearing in Julius Caesars’ Gallic Wars, where a war is won by with the help of this cipher. In fact, Julius Caesar was a big fan of Ciphers, according to Suetonius’ Lives of the Caesars’s.

This Cipher is created by shifting the letters of the alphabet a number of terms to the left. In the simplest example, you only shift once, so A goes to B, B goes to C etc, as seen in this diagram.

The Monoalphabetic Substitution Cipher

This is the name given to any substitution cipher where the cipher alphabet is either letters or symbols. A Substitution cipher. There are 40000000000000000000000000 possible outcomes. Frequency analysis invented by Al Kindi, the ‘Philosopher of Arabs’ Short texts likely to deviate from the norm. Until 1500 the codebreakers had the upper hand, was the safest form of encryption. Describe how to crack. Describe and example The key to cracking this cipher lies in the fact that some letters appear more than others, depending on the language. For example, Al Kindi found that the letters a and l appear more often that the other letters in Arabic. Likewise, in English, the most common letter in English is e, which occurs 12.7% of the time, followed by t, then a, then o. Of course, the shorter the text, the more likely the letters are to deviate from the norm! Example Challenge
